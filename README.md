# ✈ AeroLearn

> An interactive 3D web app that teaches the physics of flight, designed for curious 8th-graders (and anyone who has ever stared up at a plane and wondered *how*).

AeroLearn turns the dense, formula-heavy chapter on "flight" into something you can actually *play with*. You move a slider, the plane reacts, the force vectors grow or flip, and a short, conversational explanation tells you what just happened and why it matters.

No accounts. No ads. Open it, learn for ten minutes, close it.

---

## What You'll Learn

Each lesson is a self-contained mini-chapter. A 3D plane responds live to your input, force vectors show *what is actually happening*, and a written explanation puts it in context.

### 🔵 The 4 Forces of Flight
Lift, weight, thrust, and drag — the four forces in a constant tug-of-war whenever an aircraft is in the air. Push the **throttle** up and watch thrust grow; drag follows it. Tilt the **angle of attack** up and lift grows... until you go past 15° and the wing **stalls** — lift collapses, the plane buffets, you understand why pilots train so hard to avoid this.

### 🟢 Wings & Lift
The curved cross-section of a wing — the **airfoil** — is the whole reason flight is possible. A side-by-side 2D illustration shows how air flowing over the more-curved top travels faster and creates lower pressure, while slower air underneath creates higher pressure. The difference is *lift*. Drag the angle of attack and the airspeed and see the streamlines, pressure zones, and the lift vector respond in real time.

### 🟡 Flaps & Slow Flight
Why airliners need long runways. The lift equation says lift depends on **speed squared** — halve the speed, lift drops to a quarter. So how does a 200-tonne 747 land at 150 mph and not fall out of the sky? Flaps. Deploy them and watch the lift vector grow even at constant airspeed. Reflex them upward (negative) and the lift vector actually flips downward — exactly what happens on aerobatic aircraft pulling sustained negative-G manoeuvres.

### 🟠 Stabilisers — Built-in Balance
The "tail feathers" of an aircraft. Without them, planes would tumble like a feathered dart with the feathers ripped off. Learn how the **horizontal stabiliser** damps pitch oscillations and how the **vertical fin** keeps the nose pointing forward — all without any pilot input. Get an intuition for *static stability* (does the plane want to return to level?) and why some modern fighter jets are deliberately *unstable* for agility.

### 🔴 Ailerons & Roll
Two small flaps near the wingtips, moving in **opposite directions**, are what bank the plane into a turn. The slider deflects the ailerons; small green vectors at each wingtip show how lift increases on one side and decreases on the other; the plane visibly rolls. Once you've seen this, every plane bank you watch from a window seat will make immediate sense.

### 🟣 Rudder & Yaw
The vertical control surface at the tail swings the nose left and right. A magenta side-force vector on the rudder shows how much sideways "lift" the rudder is generating as you deflect it. Learn why pilots use the rudder *together* with ailerons in coordinated turns.

### 🟦 Elevator & Pitch
Pull back to climb, push forward to dive. The horizontal flap on the tail (elevator) is the most direct, most immediate control. See the elevator deflect, the tail-down force vector flip, and the nose rise. Also: why pitching up alone doesn't make you climb forever — you need throttle too.

### 🎮 Fly It!
All four primary controls active at the same time. Try a coordinated turn (roll + rudder + a little back-pressure), a climbing roll, a crosswind crab. The clouds tilt with your banking, drift sideways when you yaw, and fall past you when you climb. The 5-minute version of a flight simulator.

## How to Use It

- **Drag** the visualisation to rotate the camera around the plane.
- **Pinch / scroll** to zoom in and out.
- **Two-finger drag** (mobile) or **right-click drag** (desktop) to pan.
- **Tap a lesson tab** to jump between topics.
- **Tap "📖 Details"** (mobile) or scroll the lesson panel (desktop) to read the full explanation for each concept.
- **Move the sliders** and watch the vectors, surfaces, and clouds respond.

There's nothing to learn about the UI itself — everything is direct manipulation. The app works on a phone, a tablet, or a desktop browser. It works without any internet connection after the page first loads.
